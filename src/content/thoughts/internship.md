# how to do well in internships

Recently, my fellow club members and I led an internship experience panel to provide advice and tips for those who are heading off into their internships for the summer. This is a compilation of many of the advice we gave plus some that I wasn't able to fit into that panel.

# the survival kit

Let's start with the basics.

You are going to be bombarded with a lot of information throughout your internship. Setup instructions, new acronyms, random commands, you name it! You don't need to remember everything; you just need to write them down and be organized enough so you don't repeat questions and waste your time trying to remember what you did weeks ago.

## your personal knowledge base

Keep a Google doc or equivalent of your own "internal documentation". Put:
* useful resources
* previously asked questions and answers
* code snippets, commands, that one bug fix that will come back to haunt you
* slack channels, team documentation
* daily standup docs, manager meeting docs, conversation docs

It will save you so much time, and trust me, when you have deadlines you want to meet but you are stuck on the same problem you solved ages ago, it will not feel good. You only have 12 weeks. Optimize it!

For the last 20% to go above and beyond, it is good to wrap up your project nicely and leave a positive impression on your ownership and responsibleness. For example, you can document your project throughout your internship so your team can actually use it even after you leave. You can also leave good descriptive PR and commeit messages instead of your usual ".".

## the brag doc

This is a bit different than the knowledge base. A "brag doc" is a compilation of accomplishments during your stay at your company. I recommend organizing it on a weekly basis, or even on a daily basis if need be, so that you can see the incremental progress. It is useful for standups and especially for when performance reviews roll around; for interns, that would be intern midpoint/final review. 

Make sure to put down all the things you did:
* Major optimizations & metrics/impact
* Major PRs you took care of
* Big project milestones/stretch goals reached
* Positive feedback from your team
* Any other accomplishments you are proud of

This is also a great way to keep your mentor and manager in the loop on what you are doing. They are quite busy, so they might forget if you don't remind them.

But you shouldn't just be listing the positive things. Include the failures, the feedback, and the learnings from it. Everyone fails at some point, and how you recover from it will show you are unafraid to try new things and take ownership. These are good signals to send to your company/team.

I recommend asking regular feedback from your manager/mentor on how you are doing and if they have something for you to improve upon, write it down! Keep it in your brag doc and show that improvement the next meetings. They will be impressed!

## self-sufficiency

You may have seen memes around where that one intern with a singular task hands it off to their manager with 99 other tasks going on. Do not be that intern!

People say there is no such thing as a stupid question, but in reality, there are. Unless you are doing the pioneering work in your field (who makes an intern do that?), most likely the question you asked may have been solved somewhere else, by someone more experienced. 

I am not trying to say to spend your entire day grappling with a singular question and waste your time. But you can definitely avoid asking the same question twice (use that personal knowledge base!) and you can google that simple question just fine. 

# the engineering mindset

In school, you are done when the code passes the autograder (and you can spam it as many times as you want). At a job, though, that is not as simple as that. The engineering mindset is realizing you are solving a problem for a real person! To go above and beyond, think about how to improve your project from the customer's shoes, how it fits in the business, and the impact/scale at which it can operate on.

## asking (good) questions

Obviously we need to ask good questions. But how?? By asking specific questions. Ask questions where the responder will need as few follow-up questions as possible. In order to do so, the question needs to be on point with enough context. Follow the "what I've tried" format: state what the goal is, what you've tried so far, and what you think is the issue.

## communication & asking feedback

What will set you apart from all the other interns is whether you are someone that is good to work with. That means good communication skills! Communicate your work with others, form good relationships with the people around you. At the end you will get peer-reviewed for your work, so the more people that know about your work the better. Also communicate early on if you feel you are heading in the wrong direction to better use your time.

Ask feedback regularly and directly from your team, and outside your team. Ask what you can be doing better and incorporate it so they don't need to tell you twice. Get coffee chats with people outside of your team also to get an assessment of how the company culture is and advice from those who have spent much more in the industry than you.

## planning out your internship

Now, this is going to look different for a lot of people. But the general timeline for a large tech 12-week internship looks something like this:
* week 1: setup, getting used to the work
* week 2-4: design doc, figuring out project scope (ask LOTS of questions so you don't repeat your work down the line)
* week 5-9: project! Think about stretch goals and meeting that intern midpoint review (around week 6-7)
* week 10-11: wrapping up stretch goals and prepping for the intern review presentation
This is always a rough guideline; make sure to check in regularly with your manager/mentor and always try to stay ahead of their expectations!

# going above and beyond

## proactiveness

If you have nothing to do, ask for something. If they say there’s nothing for you to do, find things to do. This can be stretch goals that you think are good, or small optimizations to your project, or even looking more in depth about your team’s work and trying to understand PR requests. People want and need someone that is passionate and serious about their work, and more often times than not, you are eager to understand more deeply about your work and improve upon it if you are like that. This will transform you from a good intern to an amazing intern! This is also a very good thing to have in life in general, outside of your internship.

## being a likeable person

I am not telling you to suddenly become a huge extrovert and always have a smile on your face despite work being stressful. However, basic courtesy goes a long way. Be nice, try to be supportive of your team and be understanding if they are too busy to help sometimes. Be a team player!

On the other hand, be yourself but do be professional; you can totally be friends with your team but do not go over the line. Make sure you can differentiate between the jokes you tell your friends and your team, regardless of how close you are.

## the non-internship things

People forget to have fun in their internships because it is such a short, and for many, stressful time. But remember, you will be doing this for potentially many, many years of your life as a full-time job! Go to those fun intern events and make friends (form those connections!). Remember to have work-life balance and always evaluate if the work you are doing is something you can see yourself doing for 30 years down your career path. Just learn, try your best, and let all the wisdom and knowledge you've gathered become a strong foundation for whatever else there is to come!
